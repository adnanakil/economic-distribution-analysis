Democratic Stress Index

Tracking four empirical indicators historically associated with political extremism and democratic backsliding

December 2025 | Data Analysis

Academic research has identified several measurable factors that correlate with the rise of extremist movements and democratic erosion. This index tracks four of these indicators in the United States: economic distress, declining national pride, demographic change rates, and institutional trust.[1]

The goal is not to predict specific outcomes, but to provide a data-driven snapshot of conditions that historically precede political instability. Each component draws from peer-reviewed research on the causes of populist surges and authoritarian backsliding.[2]

Current Status: Elevated Risk Conditions

The composite index currently registers at 76.8 (out of 100), placing it in the "elevated risk" category. Notably, this is not driven by economic factors—unemployment remains relatively low at 4.4%.[3] Instead, the elevated reading comes from historic lows in national pride[4] and institutional trust,[5] combined with demographic change rates at their highest since 1920.[6]

Index Interpretation Guide

0-30: Low Risk — Stable conditions, democratic norms reinforced
30-50: Moderate Risk — Some warning indicators present
50-70: High Risk — Multiple indicators elevated simultaneously
70-100: Elevated Risk — Historical conditions associated with political instability

Methodology: The Four Components

This index combines four factors identified by academic meta-analyses as correlates of extremist political movements:

  • Economic Distress (33% weight): Unemployment rate — Research shows economic hardship increases support for anti-establishment movements[7]
  • Status Anxiety (30% weight): National pride levels — Perceived loss of national standing correlates with receptivity to "restoration" narratives[8]
  • Demographic Change Rate (17% weight): Foreign-born population percentage — Rapid demographic shifts correlate with cultural anxiety, independent of attitudes toward immigration[9]
  • Institutional Trust (20% weight): Trust in federal government — Collapse of faith in institutions creates demand for outsider candidates[10]

Weights derived from Scheiring et al. (2024) meta-analysis of 36 studies, which found economic factors explain approximately 33% of populist vote share variance, with cultural and institutional factors comprising the remainder.[1]

Important Caveats

This index has significant limitations that should inform how the data is interpreted:

Correlation ≠ Causation: These indicators are associated with historical instances of democratic stress, but they do not deterministically cause political outcomes. Many countries have experienced similar conditions without democratic collapse.

Proxy limitations: The "demographic change rate" component measures the pace of foreign-born population change, not attitudes toward immigration. These can diverge significantly—some research suggests that perception of demographic threat matters more than actual demographic change.[11]

Data gaps: Pre-2001 national pride data is estimated based on historical context, as Gallup polling on this specific question began in 2001.[4] These estimates introduce additional uncertainty.

Context dependency: The same index value can mean different things in different institutional contexts. A country with strong independent judiciary and free press faces different risks than one without these safeguards.

Component Analysis: Individual Trends

1. Economic Distress: Unemployment Rate

Current: 4.4% (September 2025)[3] — Relatively low by historical standards. Economic factors are not the primary driver of current elevated readings.

2. Status Anxiety: National Pride

Current: 41% "extremely proud" (2025)[4] — Combined "extremely/very proud" at 58%, the lowest since Gallup began tracking in 2001. Down from 87% in 2001.

3. Demographic Change: Foreign-Born Population

Current: 14.3% (2023 Census estimate)[6] — Highest since 1920 (13.2%). Note: This measures demographic change rate, not attitudes.

4. Institutional Trust: Trust in Government

Current: ~22% (2025)[5] — Near historic lows. Notably, trust has become highly partisan: 42% of Republicans vs. 31% of Democrats currently trust the federal government.

Key Insight: This Is Not an Economic Crisis

Unlike the Great Depression or 2008 financial crisis, current elevated readings are not driven by unemployment (which remains at 4.4%). Instead, the primary drivers are:

  • National pride at historic lows: Only 58% extremely/very proud (vs. 87% in 2001)[4]
  • Institutional trust collapse: ~22% trust government (vs. 77% in 1964)[5]
  • Demographic change rate: Foreign-born at highest % since 1920[6]

This pattern aligns with research showing status threat and cultural factors can be stronger drivers of populist support than economic hardship.[8][12]

What This Index Does Not Measure

Several important factors are not captured in this composite index:

  • Institutional resilience: Strength of judiciary, press freedom, civil society
  • Elite behavior: Whether political leaders respect democratic norms
  • Social cohesion: Cross-cutting identities that can reduce polarization
  • Information environment: Media fragmentation, misinformation prevalence
  • International context: External threats or support for democratic institutions

These factors can significantly modify how the measured conditions translate into political outcomes. A high index value in a country with strong institutions may pose less risk than a moderate value in a country with weak institutions.
